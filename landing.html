The four pathways, in depth
Four routes to a healthier weight — they combine, not compete

🥗Pathway 1

Dietary & lifestyle change

Diet, activity, sleep and daily habits are the foundation of weight management — the base that every other pathway is built on, and enough on its own for many people. The goal is not a short, punishing diet but a comfortable, sustainable way of eating and moving that you can keep for years. Even a gradual loss of 5–10% of your body weight can meaningfully improve blood sugar, blood pressure, cholesterol, joint pain, sleep apnoea and fatty liver.

What actually works

No single diet is magic — the best eating pattern is the one you can maintain. Most successful approaches share the same core: more protein and vegetables, more whole and less processed food, fewer sugary drinks and refined snacks, and portions matched to your needs. Crash diets tend to rebound because they are hard to sustain and can strip muscle; a gentle, steady calorie deficit combined with activity holds up far better over time.

Everyday habits that help

  • Fill half your plate with vegetables and make protein the anchor of each meal.
  • Drink water instead of juice and soft drinks — sugary drinks add calories without fullness.
  • Slow down, notice fullness, and keep tempting snacks out of easy reach.
  • Aim for at least 150 minutes of activity a week plus strengthening twice weekly — start small and build.
  • Protect 7–9 hours of sleep: short sleep raises appetite hormones and cravings.
A dietitian can tailor all of this to you — especially with diabetes, kidney disease, pregnancy, or before and after a procedure.
💊Pathway 2

Medical — weight-loss medications

Anti-obesity medications are prescription treatments that help when lifestyle change alone isn't enough. They are usually considered at a higher BMI or when weight-related conditions such as type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure or sleep apnoea are present. They are tools, not shortcuts — they work best alongside eating and activity changes, and weight often returns if they stop, so they are generally taken long term under medical review.

The main options (generic names)

  • GLP-1 medicines — semaglutide and liraglutide — mimic a gut hormone that reduces appetite and slows stomach emptying, so you feel full sooner and eat less.
  • Tirzepatide acts on two gut-hormone pathways (GIP and GLP-1) and is among the most effective medical options.
  • Orlistat blocks the absorption of some dietary fat in the gut.
  • Naltrexone–bupropion acts on appetite and cravings; phentermine is a short-term appetite suppressant.

Side effects & getting started

Doses are started low and increased slowly to limit side effects. The most common are gastrointestinal — nausea, fullness, constipation or diarrhoea — which usually ease with time and with smaller, slower, lower-fat meals. If your medicine is an injection, it is given under the skin of the abdomen, thigh or upper arm; rotating the site and using a fresh needle each time makes it easier. Your doctor screens for conditions where a medicine isn't suitable and monitors your response.

Qawam never gives doses. Record only what your doctor prescribed, and never start, stop, adjust or borrow a dose without them.
🔬Pathway 3

Endoscopic procedures

Endoscopic weight procedures are done with a flexible scope passed through the mouth — no surgical cuts and usually a quick recovery. They sit between medications and surgery in intensity, offering a meaningful step for people who want more than medication but are not ready for, or do not need, an operation.

The main procedures

  • Intragastric balloon — a soft balloon is placed in the stomach and filled, so you feel full sooner and eat less. It is temporary, usually removed after about six months, and works best paired with lifestyle change.
  • Endoscopic sleeve gastroplasty (ESG) — internal stitches reshape the stomach into a narrow tube, reducing its volume without removing any tissue.
  • Aspiration therapy — a tube allows a portion of a meal to be drained from the stomach after eating.

Recovery & results

Afterwards you follow a staged diet — clear liquids, then full liquids, purée, soft foods and finally regular textures — sipping fluids often and eating slowly. Early nausea or cramping is common and settles. As with every pathway, results depend on keeping the eating and activity habits going, so follow-up matters. Qawam includes a built-in diet-phase tracker to guide you through each stage.

🏥Pathway 4

Surgical — bariatric surgery

Bariatric (metabolic) surgery is the most effective and durable treatment for severe obesity, and it often improves or resolves type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, sleep apnoea and fatty liver. In experienced centres it is very safe, but like any operation it carries risks that your surgeon will explain in full.

The two most common operations

  • Sleeve gastrectomy — about 80% of the stomach is removed, leaving a narrow tube. You feel full on much smaller amounts, and hunger hormones fall.
  • Roux-en-Y gastric bypass — a small stomach pouch is connected to a lower part of the small intestine, reducing intake and changing gut hormones and absorption.

A lifelong partnership

Surgery is a powerful tool that requires lifelong teamwork: a pre-operative work-up and liver-shrink diet; a carefully staged post-op diet over about six to eight weeks; daily vitamins for life; protein and fluid goals to protect muscle and prevent dehydration; and regular follow-up with blood tests. Golden rules at the table are protein first, sip fluids between meals, eat slowly, stop at the first sign of fullness, and avoid sugary and fizzy drinks. Weight regain can happen if habits slip, so the eating, activity and follow-up work continues long after the operation.

After bariatric surgery, the body absorbs fewer nutrients — lifelong daily supplements (a bariatric multivitamin, B12, iron, calcium with vitamin D, and folic acid) and periodic blood tests are essential. Qawam has a daily vitamins checklist to help.
Understand weight & health
A little science makes every pathway clearer

Weight is a medical condition, not a character flaw

Excess weight is a chronic, relapsing medical condition — not a lack of willpower. Genes, hormones, sleep, stress, medications and environment all shape appetite and how the body stores energy. Because it is chronic, weight is managed over the long term, and small sustained changes beat short crash diets. Understanding this removes a lot of unnecessary guilt and helps you and your team choose tools that fit your biology, not just your motivation.

What BMI does — and doesn't — tell you

Body mass index (BMI) is your weight in kilograms divided by your height in metres squared. As a rough adult guide: under 18.5 is underweight, 18.5–24.9 is a healthy range, 25–29.9 is overweight, and 30 or above is in the obesity range (class I 30–34.9, class II 35–39.9, class III 40+). But BMI is only a screening number: it does not measure muscle, fat distribution or health directly, and cut-offs can differ for some populations. An athlete and someone with little muscle can share a BMI yet have very different health.

Why waist matters too

Waist circumference adds useful information about fat stored around the organs, which carries more health risk than fat elsewhere. Broadly, higher risk is often quoted above about 102 cm in men and 88 cm in women (some guidance uses 94 cm and 80 cm). Measure at the navel after breathing out gently, without pulling the tape tight. Watching your waist trend alongside your weight gives a fuller picture — and it sometimes improves even when the scale is stubborn.

Plateaus are normal, not failure

Weight loss almost never happens in a straight line. As you lose weight the body needs fewer calories and adapts to defend its weight, so progress slows — plateaus are expected. The day-to-day scale also swings with fluids, food, salt and sleep, which is why weekly or trend views tell you far more than a single morning. Treat lapses as data, not disaster: notice what happened and return to your routine. Keeping weight off is its own skill, and ongoing tracking is one of the strongest predictors of success.

Questions, answered
Frequently asked questions
Is Qawam a medical device or a diet plan?
No. Qawam is a non-medical health companion for personal tracking and education. It does not diagnose, predict, prescribe calories or drug doses, or replace professional care. Everything it shows is descriptive — a way to organise your journey and prepare for conversations with your healthcare team.
Which pathway is right for me?
That depends on your starting point, health conditions, previous attempts and preferences — and the pathways combine rather than compete. Broadly, lifestyle change suits everyone and is often tried first; medications are typically considered at a higher BMI or with weight-related conditions; endoscopic procedures are a less invasive step; and surgery is the most effective option for severe obesity. This decision should always be shared with a specialist team. Qawam helps you learn about each and prepare good questions.
Do the GLP-1 medications mean I don't need to change my diet?
No — medicines work best alongside eating and activity changes, not instead of them. They reduce appetite, which makes healthy eating easier, but protecting muscle with enough protein and strength activity, and building habits you can keep, is what makes results last. Weight often returns if a medicine stops, so lifestyle remains the foundation throughout.
What happens to my data?
Everything you log stays on your device by default — there is no account required to use Qawam. You can export a copy anytime, or permanently delete everything from the More tab. When you generate a doctor summary, you choose when and with whom to share it.
Can I use Qawam after surgery or an endoscopic procedure?
Yes — that's exactly what the post-op journey is for. It walks you through the staged diet from clear liquids to regular textures, lets you mark your current stage, and gives you a daily vitamins checklist so lifelong supplements become a habit. Follow the exact timing your own team gives you.
Is it free, and does it work offline?
Yes to both. Qawam is completely free and works as an installable app: open it in your browser or add it to your home screen, and it keeps working offline. It is fully bilingual in English and Arabic with right-to-left support.
